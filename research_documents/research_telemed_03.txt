Despite its obvious benefits and rapid global adoption, telemedicine continues to face substantial implementation barriers related to digital literacy, infrastructure, and policy. A significant portion of the population, particularly the elderly and socioeconomically disadvantaged groups, lack the necessary access to reliable high-speed internet, smartphones, or computers. Even when hardware is available, inadequate digital literacy skills can make navigating complex virtual healthcare platforms daunting and ineffective. This digital divide threatens to exacerbate existing health inequities rather than resolve them. Additionally, the regulatory landscape governing telehealth is fragmented and often outdated. Policies determining insurance reimbursement coverage, state licensing requirements for cross-border medical practice, and the prescribing of medications online vary widely across different jurisdictions. This lack of standardization complicates the operations of telehealth providers and creates confusion for patients seeking coverage. For virtual care practices to become universally established and sustainable, policymakers must enact comprehensive reforms that mandate equitable reimbursement parity, invest in broadband infrastructure for rural areas, and standardize medical licensing across regions, ensuring that telemedicine's promises are accessible to all.
